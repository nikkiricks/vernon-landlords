üìö Organizing Principles for Housing Justice

ü§ù Build Collective Power

Individual problems require collective solutions. Housing issues affect entire communities - organize with your neighbors, not just for them.

  • Start with one-on-one conversations with neighbors
  • Form tenant associations and neighborhood groups
  • Connect with existing housing justice organizations
  • Use data to identify shared concerns and common targets

üîç Follow the Money

Housing is a human right, not a commodity. Track ownership patterns to understand how speculation drives displacement.

  • Identify large portfolio owners and corporate entities
  • Research property management companies and their practices
  • Map connections between owners, banks, and political donors
  • Expose below-market purchases that may indicate speculation

‚öñÔ∏è Target Decision Makers

Power concedes nothing without a demand. Identify who has the power to make changes and pressure them strategically.

  • City commissioners and housing bureau officials
  • Property owners and management companies
  • Banks and financial institutions enabling speculation
  • Developers and real estate lobby groups

üèõÔ∏è Make the Political Personal

Housing policy affects real people. Connect data trends to lived experiences and human stories.

  • Share tenant stories of displacement and rent increases
  • Document code violations and habitability issues
  • Connect ownership data to specific tenant experiences
  • Make abstract policies concrete through personal impact

üå± Think Long-term

Systemic change requires sustained organizing. Build infrastructure for ongoing campaigns, not just single victories.

  • Develop leadership among directly affected people
  • Create sustainable organizational structures
  • Build alliances across neighborhoods and issues
  • Plan for policy implementation and enforcement

‚úä Center Racial and Economic Justice

Housing inequality isn't accidental. Address how racism and classism shape housing access and displacement patterns.

  • Prioritize leadership of Black, Indigenous, and communities of color
  • Analyze displacement patterns through racial and economic lens
  • Connect housing to broader struggles for community control
  • Challenge exclusionary zoning and development patterns

üå≤ Portland Housing Justice Context

üìñ Historical Context: How We Got Here

1950s-1970s

Urban Renewal and Displacement

The destruction of Albina - Portland's historic Black neighborhood - through urban renewal projects displaced thousands of Black families and destroyed generational wealth. This set the stage for ongoing gentrification patterns.

1990s-2000s

The Growth Machine

Portland's "livability" brand attracted new residents and investment, driving up land values. Exclusionary zoning preserved single-family neighborhoods while concentrating affordable housing in specific areas.

2010s

Tech Boom and Speculation

Corporate investors and out-of-state buyers began purchasing Portland properties as investment vehicles. Airbnb removed units from housing market. Rents increased faster than wages.

2020-Present

Housing Crisis Acceleration

COVID-19 intensified housing insecurity. Wall Street investors increased purchases. Houselessness reached crisis levels. Tenant organizing and mutual aid networks expanded in response.

üåü Vision: Community-Controlled Housing

Housing justice isn't just about stopping bad landlords - it's about building a future where communities control their housing and everyone has a stable, dignified home. Here's what that could look like:

üèòÔ∏è Social Housing

Publicly-owned housing for all income levels

  • City-owned housing developed for community benefit, not profit
  • Mixed-income developments that avoid concentrating poverty
  • Tenant management and cooperative ownership models
  • Long-term affordability without displacement risk
Examples: Vienna's public housing serves 60% of residents across all income levels. Barcelona is buying buildings to convert to public housing.

ü§ù Community Land Trusts

Permanently affordable housing controlled by residents

  • Community ownership removes land from speculation
  • Resident ownership with resale restrictions maintain affordability
  • Democratic governance by residents and community members
  • Wealth building for residents without displacement risk
Examples: Burlington, VT has preserved affordable housing through CLTs since the 1980s. Champlain Housing Trust serves 3,000+ families.

üèõÔ∏è Tenant Protection Policies

Strong regulations that prioritize housing stability

  • Just cause eviction protections and strong rent stabilization
  • Right to organize without retaliation
  • Mandatory habitability standards with tenant enforcement rights
  • Tenant opportunity to purchase when properties are sold
Examples: Berlin's rent cap (until overturned). Richmond, CA's just cause and rent control. Washington State's statewide just cause law.

üí∞ Public Investment

Resources directed toward housing as public good

  • Real estate transfer taxes fund affordable housing development
  • Inclusionary zoning requires affordable units in new development
  • Public banking keeps housing investment local
  • Land value capture funds community-controlled development
Examples: Seattle's employer tax funds homeless services. San Francisco's transfer tax generates $44M annually for affordable housing.

‚öñÔ∏è Speculation Controls

Policies that prioritize housing over investment returns

  • Vacancy taxes penalize speculation and warehousing
  • Corporate ownership limits prevent Wall Street control
  • Right of first refusal gives tenants and community priority
  • Speculation taxes on rapid resale discourage flipping
Examples: Vancouver's empty homes tax reduced vacancy. Leipzig, Germany limits corporate ownership to preserve affordability.

üó≥Ô∏è Democratic Control

Community participation in housing decisions

  • Participatory budgeting for housing investments
  • Community benefits agreements for large developments
  • Tenant representation on housing authority boards
  • Community ownership of development decisions
Examples: Chicago's participatory budgeting allocates $1M annually through resident voting. Boston's community land trust has resident board control.
